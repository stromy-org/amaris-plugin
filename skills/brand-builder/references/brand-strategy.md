# Brand Strategy Reference

## Brand Archetypes (Jung's 12)

Brand archetypes are the single biggest lever for creative diversity. Different archetypes for similar industries yield radically different brands. Present **3-4 candidate archetypes** — include at least one non-obvious match.

### The 12 Archetypes

**ORDER quadrant — structure, control, stability:**

| Archetype | Core desire | Voice | Visual direction | Example brands |
|-----------|------------|-------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Ruler** | Control, power | Commanding, authoritative, premium | Dark palettes, structured layouts, strong typography, gold/silver accents | Mercedes-Benz, Rolex, Microsoft |
| **Caregiver** | Service, protection | Warm, reassuring, selfless | Soft colors, rounded shapes, nurturing imagery, open layouts | Johnson & Johnson, UNICEF, Volvo |
| **Creator** | Innovation, self-expression | Imaginative, expressive, non-conforming | Bold color choices, asymmetric layouts, artistic type, texture-rich | Adobe, Lego, Apple |

**FREEDOM quadrant — adventure, liberation, transformation:**

| Archetype | Core desire | Voice | Visual direction | Example brands |
|-----------|------------|-------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Explorer** | Discovery, freedom | Rugged, independent, adventurous | Earth tones, outdoor imagery, textured surfaces, expansive compositions | Jeep, Patagonia, National Geographic |
| **Rebel/Outlaw** | Revolution, disruption | Provocative, bold, counterculture | High contrast, dark palettes, edgy typography, unexpected compositions | Harley-Davidson, Virgin, Diesel |
| **Magician** | Transformation | Visionary, mystical, inspirational | Deep purples/blues, gradients, ethereal imagery, dynamic motion | Disney, Tesla, Dyson |

**EGO quadrant — mastery, belonging, enjoyment:**

| Archetype | Core desire | Voice | Visual direction | Example brands |
|-----------|------------|-------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Hero** | Mastery, courage | Confident, direct, empowering | Strong colors, angular shapes, action imagery, bold sans-serif | Nike, FedEx, BMW |
| **Everyman** | Belonging, connection | Friendly, honest, humble | Accessible colors, clean layouts, relatable imagery | IKEA, Target, Budweiser |
| **Jester** | Enjoyment, humor | Playful, witty, irreverent | Bright/saturated colors, playful type, unexpected pairings | M&M's, Old Spice, Ben & Jerry's |

**SOCIAL quadrant — intimacy, understanding, safety:**

| Archetype | Core desire | Voice | Visual direction | Example brands |
|-----------|------------|-------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Lover** | Intimacy, beauty | Sensual, warm, indulgent | Rich warm colors, luxurious textures, elegant serif type, intimate imagery | Chanel, Godiva, Victoria's Secret |
| **Sage** | Understanding, truth | Authoritative, analytical, informative | Clean layouts, data-forward, muted sophisticated colors, editorial serif type | BBC, Google, Harvard |
| **Innocent** | Safety, simplicity | Optimistic, simple, honest | Light/pastel palettes, clean whitespace, rounded sans-serif, natural imagery | Coca-Cola, Dove, Whole Foods |

### Archetype Selection Process (interactive)

1. Based on the brief, **shuffle** the archetype list and propose 3-4 candidates (always include at least one non-obvious match alongside expected choices)
2. Present each with: name, core desire, 1-line voice description, 3 example brands, visual direction hint
3. Ask: "Which resonates most? You can pick a primary and optionally a secondary."
4. The combination (e.g., Sage + Creator, Hero + Rebel) creates unique brand personality

**Volatility mechanism:** When selecting candidates, don't always propose the "default" archetype for an industry. A consulting firm is not always a Sage — it could be a Hero (empowering clients), a Magician (transforming businesses), or even an Explorer (charting new territory).

## Brand Pyramid

Quick-fill exercise to ground strategy before taglines:

```
Level 5 (apex):  Brand Essence — one word or phrase that IS the brand
Level 4:         Brand Personality — 5 traits (adjectives)
Level 3:         Emotional Benefits — how people FEEL (empowered, confident, relieved)
Level 2:         Functional Benefits — what you DO for them (save time, reduce risk)
Level 1 (base):  Features & Attributes — what you ARE (24/7 support, AI-powered)
```

Fill bottom-up with the user, confirm top-down.

## Brand Essence Framework

The brand essence is a single sentence capturing the soul of the company:

```
[BRAND] [verb: enables/orchestrates/delivers/builds/transforms/cultivates/ignites]
[what they do in specific terms]
so that [target audience] can [outcome they care about].
```

The verb should match the archetype — "orchestrates" for Ruler/Sage, "unleashes" for Rebel, "cultivates" for Caregiver, "ignites" for Creator.

## Positioning Statement

Internal-facing, never published:

```
For [target audience with specificity],
[BRAND] is the [category/type of company]
that [key differentiator] —
delivering [concrete value proposition].
```

## Tagline Development

Generate **6-8 tagline options** across diverse directions. **Shuffle** which directions you lead with — don't always present them in the same order.

| Direction | Strategy | Example pattern |
|-----------|----------|-----------------|
| **Functional** | Describes what you do | "Intelligence, Orchestrated." |
| **Aspirational** | Describes the outcome | "Conduct Your Edge." |
| **Descriptive** | Names the value | "Engineered Clarity." |
| **Positional** | Claims territory | "Where Process Meets Precision." |
| **Provocative** | Challenges status quo | "Stop Guessing. Start Knowing." |
| **Metaphorical** | Uses imagery/analogy | "The Compass for Complexity." |
| **Rhythmic** | Musical/memorable cadence | "Think Sharp. Move Fast." |
| **Question-based** | Engages with a question | "What Could Your Data Tell You?" |

**Tagline rules:**
- Maximum 4 words (ideally 2-3)
- Must work as a standalone phrase
- Should translate well multilingually
- Avoid cliches: "innovative solutions", "next-level", "reimagining"
- Test: could a competitor use this tagline? If yes, too generic
- Test: say it aloud — does it flow naturally?
- Test: picture it on a business card — does it fit and look right?

### Tagline Workshop (interactive)

1. Present 6-8 tagline options rendered in HTML with the brand's likely display font (or a similar serif/sans from the font repertoire)
2. Show each tagline in multiple contexts: standalone, below logo placeholder, as email signature line, on business card mockup
3. Ask user to react: "love", "interesting", "not right"
4. For favorites, generate 3-4 refinements (variations in rhythm, word choice, emphasis)
5. Confirm the winning tagline before proceeding

**Volatility mechanism:** Randomly select 5 of the 8 directions to lead with. The remaining 3 are still available but not presented first. This means the same brief won't always get the same tagline styles.

## Brand Personality

Select exactly 5 traits. Each should be:
- An adjective, not a noun
- Specific, not generic ("unflinching" not "strong")
- Differentiating — at least 2 that competitors wouldn't claim
- Aligned with the chosen archetype

### Personality Spectrums (interactive)

Present 5 slider-style spectrums in an HTML visualization. Ask the user to place the brand:

```
Formal      ●————————————○  Casual
Traditional ○————●————————○  Innovative
Reserved    ○————————●————○  Expressive
Analytical  ○————————————●○  Intuitive
Established ○——●——————————○  Rebellious
```

The spectrum positions inform trait selection. A brand at "Innovative + Expressive + Rebellious" gets very different traits than "Traditional + Reserved + Established."

## Voice Guidelines

Structure voice rules as imperatives with examples:

```
Write like [analogy — who is this person?].
[3-5 specific rules with rationale]
```

### Voice Rule Patterns

| Rule | Good for | Example |
|------|----------|---------|
| "Conclusions first" | Analytical brands | Lead with the answer, then support |
| "Short sentences" | Authoritative brands | Never more than 20 words per sentence |
| "Numbers over adjectives" | Data-driven brands | "23 clusters" not "many groups" |
| "Active voice only" | Action-oriented brands | "We analyzed" not "An analysis was conducted" |
| "No jargon theater" | Trust-focused brands | Name the tool, not the buzzword |
| "Questions welcome" | Approachable brands | "Not sure? Let's figure it out together." |
| "One idea per paragraph" | Clarity-focused brands | Never stack multiple arguments |
| "Show restraint" | Premium brands | Say less, mean more — luxury whispers |
| "Name the tension" | Provocative brands | "Everyone says X. We disagree." |
| "Stories over stats" | Empathetic brands | Lead with the person, add data to support |

### Voice Test (interactive)

Write the same message (e.g., announcing a new service offering) in 3 different voices derived from the archetype:
1. Voice A: most expected/natural for the archetype
2. Voice B: slightly edgier or more distinctive
3. Voice C: unexpected but plausible interpretation

Present all 3 in a side-by-side HTML comparison. Ask the user which voice feels right.

## Do/Don't Pairs

Always provide 2-3 do/don't pairs showing the voice in action. Write real copy the brand would publish:

```
DO:   [Example of brand voice done right — specific, with industry context]
DON'T: [Same message written in the wrong voice — generic, buzzwordy, or off-brand]
```

Make these genuinely useful: real sentences, real scenarios, real tone differences. Not abstract — concrete.
